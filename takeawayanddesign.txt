Design ideas:

1. Price Distribution Box Plot 
Our first visualization is a box plot that provides an 
overview of nightly Airbnb price distributions across 10 European cities. We place it at the 
beginning so the audience can quickly understand how markets differ before exploring why. 
The primary mark is box and whisker, and the channels are horizontal position for price and 
vertical position for city, sorted by median price so viewers can immediately rank markets. 
A single color is used since room type is not encoded here — the focus is on city-level 
comparison. Tooltips are included because box plots are difficult to read precisely at a 
glance.

2. Affordability Scatter Plot 
The second visualization extends the city-level overview by 
examining whether price differences correlate with local wage levels. A scatter plot is 
appropriate because both salary and price are continuous quantitative variables and the 
task is to identify a correlation pattern. The mark is point, and the primary channels are 
horizontal position for salary and vertical position for price. Color encodes a derived 
variable, the affordability ratio, on a red-to-green diverging scale so viewers can 
immediately identify which cities are most and least affordable relative to local wages 
without calculating anything. Tooltips are included because color communicates pattern but 
cannot give precise values.

3. Faceted Area Chart 
The third visualization explores how distance from the city center 
interacts with room type to determine price. We chose a faceted area chart because placing 
all three room types in one chart would cause overlapping curves that obscure individual 
slopes. The mark is area, and the channels are horizontal position for distance and vertical 
position for average price. The chart is faceted by room type so each decay curve is 
readable on its own. Color encodes room type within each panel to reinforce category 
identity. A brush selection interaction is included so viewers can isolate any distance 
range and see the average price and percentage of listings in that window update dynamically 
below.

4. Heatmap with Linked Bar Chart 
The fourth visualization tests whether metro proximity 
independently influences price beyond city center distance. A heatmap is appropriate because 
it encodes three variables simultaneously in a compact grid. The mark is rectangle, and the 
channels are vertical position for city and horizontal position for the distance band and 
room type combination. Color intensity on a yellow-to-red scale encodes average price 
because the grid structure makes position unavailable for the price variable. A 
click-to-filter interaction links the heatmap to a bar chart below, which shows how all 
cities compare for any selected distance band and room type. The heatmap gives the broad 
overview and the bar chart gives the precise city-level comparison the heatmap alone cannot 
provide.

5. Grouped Bar Chart with Toggle 
The fifth visualization examines whether weekend price 
premiums correspond to any change in guest satisfaction. A grouped bar chart is 
appropriate because bar height is the most accurate channel for magnitude comparison across 
discrete categories, and grouping by day type makes the weekday-to-weekend difference 
immediate per city. The mark is bar, and the channels are horizontal position for city, bar 
height for the metric value, and color for day type. A radio toggle switches the y-axis 
between price and satisfaction views, allowing viewers to directly test whether cities 
charging more on weekends also score higher on satisfaction without needing to 
cross-reference two separate charts.
